Operations March 28, 2026 6 min read

Why operational knowledge disappears — and what to do before it does

The most valuable knowledge in any infrastructure organization rarely lives in a system. It lives in people. Here is how to start closing that gap before the next wave of retirements.

There is a pattern playing out across utilities, municipalities, and infrastructure operators that most organizations are aware of but few are acting on with any urgency. An experienced engineer or operator retires. Their replacement is hired. And somewhere in that transition, a significant amount of operational knowledge simply disappears.

Not all of it. The documented procedures survive. The formal training gets passed along. But the knowledge that actually makes operations run well — the judgment calls, the workarounds, the early warning signs that only come from years of watching a particular system — that knowledge is almost never captured, and almost never transferred effectively.

What gets lost and why

The knowledge that disappears in workforce transitions tends to fall into a few categories. The first is contextual knowledge: why a particular process works the way it does, what has been tried before, what constraints shaped the current approach. This kind of knowledge rarely makes it into documentation because it feels like history rather than instruction.

The second is exception knowledge: how to handle the situations that fall outside normal procedures. Experienced operators have a mental library of edge cases and failure modes that took years to build. Formal procedures rarely capture this in any useful way.

The third, and perhaps most valuable, is early signal knowledge: the ability to recognize that something is about to go wrong before the systems flag it. This is pattern recognition built from experience, and it is nearly impossible to document in a way that transfers effectively.

The most dangerous gap is not the one that shows up in the org chart. It is the one that shows up three years after someone retires, when no one can explain why a system behaves the way it does.

Why documentation alone does not work

The standard organizational response to knowledge risk is documentation. Write it down before someone leaves. This instinct is correct but insufficient. Documentation captures what people know they know. It does not capture what they know but have never thought to articulate — the vast majority of practical operational knowledge.

There is also a timing problem. Organizations typically start thinking about knowledge capture when someone has already announced their retirement. At that point, you have weeks or months to extract knowledge that took decades to accumulate. The math does not work.

Effective knowledge transfer requires an ongoing process, not a departure protocol. It requires systems and workflows that make knowledge capture a natural part of how work gets done, rather than a separate exercise that competes with the actual work.

A more practical approach

The organizations that handle this best tend to share a few characteristics. They treat knowledge capture as an operational function, not an HR function. They use structured processes for capturing decision rationale alongside decisions, not just the decisions themselves. And they build workflows that create knowledge artifacts as a byproduct of work, rather than as an additional step after the work is done.

In practice, this means:

  • Capturing the reasoning behind decisions at the point when they are made, not retrospectively
  • Creating structured handoff processes that surface tacit knowledge explicitly
  • Building systems that make it easy to annotate procedures with exception cases as they are encountered
  • Designing workflows that pair experienced operators with less experienced ones on complex tasks, with explicit knowledge transfer as a goal

None of this is novel in principle. The gap is in execution — specifically, in having systems and workflows that make these practices sustainable at the pace of real operational work.

Where to start

The most useful first step is usually an honest assessment of where your organization is most vulnerable. Not in general terms, but specifically: which roles, which systems, which workflows carry the highest concentration of tacit knowledge with the highest turnover risk in the next three to five years?

That assessment tends to surface two or three areas where the risk is both high and actionable. Starting there — with focused effort on a single workflow or role — is almost always more productive than a broad knowledge management initiative.

The organizations that make the most progress on this problem are the ones that treat it as an operational challenge rather than a change management challenge. The question is not how to get people to change their behavior. The question is how to design the work itself so that knowledge capture happens as a natural part of doing it.
